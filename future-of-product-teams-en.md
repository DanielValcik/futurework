## Executive Summary

Artificial intelligence isn't just changing our tools — it's changing how teams are organized, how decisions are made, and how products are created.

This document combines insights from leading global sources **(SVPG, Reforge, PwC, Gartner, IBM, Insight Partners, IMD, Microsoft Design, Vercel)** with our own vision and concept of hub-based architecture with Orchestrators.

> **Key takeaway:** The future belongs neither to purely traditional triads (PM + Designer + Tech Lead) nor to fully autonomous solopreneurs. It belongs to **smaller but more powerful teams** operating around shared AI hubs — **a Design Hub, Content Hub, and Insights Hub** — led by Orchestrators who combine product thinking, design sensibility, and technical literacy.

## 1. Current State: Where the Industry Stands

### 1.1 The Traditional Model: PM + Designer + Engineers

Most product organizations today operate on the model described by Marty Cagan at Silicon Valley Product Group (SVPG). An **empowered product team** consists of a product manager, a product designer, and engineers led by a tech lead. The team is focused on **outcomes**, not output, and is empowered — it has the authority to find the best solutions to the problems it's been entrusted with.

This model worked brilliantly in the cloud era. Reforge's analysis of AI-native teams reminds us that cloud computing didn't just emerge as a new technology — it **catalyzed an entirely new ecosystem of product development**: cross-functional pods, continuous deployment, product-led growth, freemium models. AI now represents exactly this kind of paradigm shift.

### 1.2 Trezor Today

Trezor currently operates in **domain-based teams**: Growth (onboarding, acquisition), Wallet, Trade, Earn, Foundation, and others. Each team has a PM, a designer, and a tech lead with engineers. It's a classic empowered model. The CEO wants to go the AI route, which opens a fundamental question: **how must this model adapt?**

### 1.3 What's Changing Right Now (2025–2026)

The industry is at a tipping point:

- **Gartner** predicts that by the end of 2026, **40% of enterprise applications** will integrate task-specific AI agents (up from less than 5% in 2025). By 2030, **80% of large engineering teams** will be reorganized into smaller, AI-augmented units.
- **Reforge** documents that **94% of product professionals** already use AI regularly and nearly half deeply integrate it into their workflows, yielding savings of **1–2 hours per day**.
- **PwC** introduces the concept of an **"AI Studio"** — a centralized hub that brings together reusable tech components, frameworks for assessing use cases, a sandbox for testing, and deployment protocols.
- **IBM** describes three key shifts: AI is moving from individual usage to **team and workflow orchestration**. Reasoning capabilities are shifting AI from a passive assistant to an **active collaborator**. And the democratization of agent creation is putting power in the hands of everyday business users, not just developers.

## 2. Research: How Leading Thinkers See the Future

### 2.1 SVPG (Marty Cagan): A Vision for Product Teams

Marty Cagan, in his landmark article "A Vision for Product Teams" (2025), openly states that generative AI will bring **significant disruption** to PM, designer, and engineer roles. At the same time, he emphatically warns against two extremes: those who claim nothing substantial will change, and those who predict the end of all these roles.

**Cagan's core argument:** Thanks to AI-powered tools, the cognitive load on teams decreases, which means the **scope of responsibility for a single team can increase dramatically**. This leads to:

- Fewer teams with smaller sizes
- Greater autonomy and impact
- Better empowerment, communication, coordination, and satisfaction

**Critical warning:** Cagan does not support the concept of "solopreneurs" or merging all roles into one. He argues that no-code and low-code tools have existed for years and results are rarely what anyone hoped for. *"It's not the tool; it's what you choose to do with that tool. The majority of people that believe all these roles are about to be automated are just telling me that they are not yet aware of what they don't know."*

> **SVPG view:** Smaller teams, bigger scope, distinct competencies preserved (PM, design, engineering). AI will fundamentally change several roles but won't eliminate the need for deep expertise.

### 2.2 Reforge (Brian Balfour): AI-Native Teams

Brian Balfour at Reforge approaches the topic through a **historical analogy with the cloud revolution**. Cloud didn't just add new tools — it completely redefined how we work. AI will do the same. Interestingly, he says that much of what AI enables **resembles or maintains how great early-stage startups operate**.

In early startups, something magical happens: teams ship more with fewer people. There are super tight feedback loops between builders and customers. Everyone does a bit of everything. As the company grows, that magic disappears. **AI can maintain the magic of early-stage startups even at scale.**

Reforge also emphasizes **"product intuition"** as the key differentiator. Scott Belsky (former CPO of Adobe): *"Until now, skills have been a major differentiator. In the age of AI, taste will become more important than skills as much of skill-based work is offloaded to compute."*

### 2.3 PwC: AI Studio as an Organizational Model

PwC's 2026 AI predictions introduce the concept of an **"AI Studio"** as a centralized hub for developing and deploying AI agents. This hub contains:

- Reusable technology components
- Frameworks for assessing use cases
- A sandbox for prototyping and testing
- Deployment protocols and skilled people
- A structure linking business goals to AI capabilities

PwC strongly warns against a "grassroots" approach where teams experiment in isolation. *This approach may generate impressive adoption numbers but seldom produces meaningful business outcomes.* Instead, they recommend **"go narrow and deep"** — select key workflows and drive transformation top-down with enterprise muscle.

PwC also describes an **orchestration layer**, where "manager" agents delegate tasks to specialized agents and assemble results. Their "Agent OS" serves as a control plane with a unified dashboard, governance, and centralized planning.

### 2.4 Insight Partners & IMD: The End of the Experimental Phase

**Insight Partners** states that by 2026, the experimental phase of enterprise AI is over. AI is moving from feature to **foundational infrastructure**. Discovery and delivery have merged into a single continuous loop. Static quarterly roadmaps are giving way to dynamic portfolios that adjust continuously.

The value of a PM is no longer measured by the volume of documentation produced, but by the **clarity of strategic intent behind each decision**. Product bets are increasingly treated as live experiments.

**IMD** adds that the winners won't be those deploying the most models, but those who **reinvent how decisions, teams, and accountability are organized around AI**. And Gartner predicts a gradual compression of middle management — a manager who used to oversee 10 analysts will lead a smaller team supported by AI agents.

### 2.5 Design Systems as Intelligent Ecosystems

Ryda Rashid describes the evolution of design systems in 2026: we're moving from style guides through living systems to **intelligent ecosystems**. 2025 was the year of "AI-assisted design," 2026 is the year of **AI-enforced consistency, multi-brand orchestration, and operational clarity**.

AI in design systems will:

- Detect **design drift** before it spreads
- Suggest what to share and what to fork
- Classify components automatically based on usage patterns
- Predict breaking changes
- Figma's MCP Dev Mode already allows AI agents to read tokens and component metadata across brands

### 2.6 Klarna: A Cautionary Tale of Overly Aggressive AI Transformation

Klarna is **the most important case study of 2025 as an anti-pattern**. CEO Sebastian Siemiatkowski aggressively promoted AI as a replacement for people: workforce dropped from 5,500 to 3,400, and an AI chatbot reportedly replaced the work of 700 customer service agents.

**Six months later:** Customer satisfaction dropped dramatically. Response quality was inconsistent and generic. Customers complained about a lack of empathy and AI's inability to handle more complex problems.

Siemiatkowski eventually admitted: *"We went too far."* Klarna is now transitioning to a hybrid model. According to an **IBM survey**, only one in four AI projects delivers its promised ROI, and just 16% are scaled across the enterprise.

> **Lesson for Trezor:** Klarna replaced people with AI. The right approach is to **change how people work with AI** — augmentation, not substitution. Hub architecture ensures this because hubs serve people, they don't replace them.

### 2.7 Vercel & Guillermo Rauch: Democratizing Building

Guillermo Rauch, CEO of Vercel and creator of Next.js, demonstrates how AI changes team dynamics in practice. Vercel, with 600 employees, uses v0 and Cursor internally so that **designers, marketers, and PMs can contribute directly to code** through AI.

Rauch identifies **three essential skills** for the new world:

1. **Clear definition of product intent**
2. **Effective coaching of AI** through iteration
3. **Quick problem-solving** when AI gets stuck

He also emphasizes the concept of **"exposure hours"** — intentional time the entire team spends with real users and the real product, not just dashboards and reports.

While Rauch says "everyone's an engineer now," he also emphasizes that deep understanding of software systems **remains valuable** — AI gives more people the ability to build, but output quality depends on the **taste** and **product sense** of the person directing the AI.

### 2.8 Microsoft Design: Design Systems for a Non-Deterministic World

Microsoft introduced a fundamental shift in design system thinking through its article "Design Systems for the AI Era." The core thesis: traditional design systems address visual consistency, but an **AI-first design system must support adaptability, context, and memory**.

AI introduces **non-deterministic outputs** — every output is slightly different, conversational, and context-dependent. Microsoft Design describes a shift:

- From **icons and static containers** → to **actions and adaptive components**
- From **fixed layouts** → to **dynamic spaces** that adapt to user needs in real time
- From **binary states** (on/off) → to **spectrums** (AI-infused sliders, adaptive controls)

Conversation blends with GUI, and the design system must account for both.

> **For Trezor:** The Design Hub isn't just a "smart component library" — it's a system that accounts for the non-determinism of AI outputs and provides **guardrails and flexibility simultaneously**.

### 2.9 Composite Squads: The Spotify Model Evolved for the AI Era

CIO.com published the **"Composite Squads"** concept in 2025 — a reimagined version of the Spotify model for the human-AI enterprise. This is an essential evolution, especially relevant for Trezor because Trezor already operates in domain-based teams similar to squads.

**Four pillars of the model:**

**Composite Squads** — Hybrid units where human members collaborate with AI copilots and embedded agents. Each squad member has one or more AI copilots. Two types of agents exist:
- **Squad-wide agents** (sprint orchestrator bot, retrospective summarizer)
- **Individual agents** paired with specific people

**Liquid Workflows** — Dynamic redistribution of work between humans and AI agents based on current needs. Insights stream continuously. Developers receive context-aware suggestions before writing code. Legal checks are embedded into the development process, not overlaid.

**Cognitive Mesh Tribes** — Connective tissue where tribes share knowledge and context through AI. Shared intelligence across squads.

**Agentic Governance** — A governance layer defining what AI agents can and cannot do autonomously, where human-in-the-loop is required.

**Real-world results:** In a digital banking firm, a composite squad reduced time-to-market by 35%, post-release bugs by 50%, and simultaneously increased personalization of customer experiences.

> **Composite Squads are a direct framework for the hub-based model:** Squads (domain teams) operate as composite units with AI agents, while hubs (Design, Content, Insights) function as **cognitive mesh — shared intelligence across squads**.

## 3. Vision: Hub-Based Architecture for AI-Native Teams

Based on research and hands-on experience at Trezor, we propose a hybrid model that combines the best of both worlds: it preserves the principles of empowered teams (SVPG), adds shared AI hubs, and enables role convergence toward the Orchestrator. Daniel Valčík published these ideas in March 2025 in his Medium article "Designing Tomorrow."

### 3.1 Three Shared Hubs

#### Design Hub

Functions as a **"design system for AI tools."** It contains several layers:

1. **Base Rules Layer** — Defines what tools we use, when, why, and with what constraints. Contains foundational design tokens (colors, typography, spacing, grid) that are readable by AI agents.

2. **Smart Components Layer** — Components that "know" when to be used, which component they pair with and which they don't, in what context and with what variations. This layer is enforced by AI agents (design drift detection, automatic validation).

3. **Generative Templates Layer** — AI can generate new layouts and flows within defined parameters based on the Orchestrator's brief. Outputs go through automatic validation against the rules.

4. **Non-Deterministic Design Layer** *(new — inspired by Microsoft Fluent for AI Era)* — Components that adapt in real time to user context, conversational inputs, and AI-generated content. The hub defines **guardrails for non-deterministic outputs** — how far the UI can adapt and where the hard boundaries are.

> **Parallel with PwC:** The Design Hub is the equivalent of PwC's "AI Studio" but specifically for design — it brings together reusable components, evaluation frameworks, and a sandbox for generation.

#### Content Hub

Responsible for all communication in the product and around it. Knows precisely what tone we speak in, when to use which phrasing, and where. Understands user flows and briefs from the Orchestrator, so it can:

- Assemble all **communication scenarios in the UI** (onboarding copy, error messages, success states, empty states, tooltips)
- Flag where **FAQ, knowledge base, in-app help, and release notes** need updating
- Ensure **consistency across all touchpoints** and language versions

#### Insights Hub

Central repository of all user research, data, and knowledge. Based on the Orchestrator's request (prompt), it can:

- Provide **preliminary validation** of whether an idea has a real foundation in data and user needs
- Synthesize qualitative (interviews, feedback) and quantitative data (analytics, metrics) to identify patterns
- Function as **"organizational memory"** — eliminating knowledge loss during personnel changes
- Facilitate **"exposure hours"** *(new — inspired by Vercel)* — ensure that Orchestrators spend intentional time with real users, not just dashboards. The hub organizes and synthesizes these direct contacts.

> **Parallel with Reforge:** The Insights Hub directly aligns with Reforge Insights, which connects fragmented feedback into centralized analysis and links qualitative findings with quantitative metrics.

### 3.2 The Orchestrator: A New Kind of Product Leader

The Orchestrator is a role that combines the best of PM, designer, and technical leader. **This is not a solopreneur** — it's a leader of a smaller team who can:

1. Define the problem and strategic intent *(PM competency)*
2. Design and validate the user experience *(design competency)*
3. Effectively prompt and direct AI tools for implementation *(technical literacy)*
4. Communicate with shared hubs and leverage their capabilities
5. Be accountable for **outcomes, not output**

This aligns with both Cagan (preserving deep expertise but in a smaller team with bigger scope) and Reforge (product intuition and taste as the key differentiator).

> **The Taste > Skills Principle:** Scott Belsky (former CPO of Adobe) and Guillermo Rauch (CEO of Vercel) agree — in the AI era, "taste" (judgment, discernment, design sense) will become more important than technical skills. **For Trezor, this means: when looking for Orchestrators, prioritize product sense and design taste, not necessarily the strongest technical profiles.**

## 4. Proposed Model for Trezor

### 4.1 From Domain Teams to Hub-Augmented Teams

Trezor's current structure (Growth, Wallet, Trade, Earn, Foundation) doesn't need to be dismantled — domain-based division remains meaningful for problem ownership. **What changes:**

| Dimension | Today | Target State |
|-----------|-------|-------------|
| **Team size** | PM + Designer + Tech Lead + 3–4 engs | Orchestrator + Tech Lead + 1–2 engs |
| **Number of teams** | ~6 domain teams | Potentially more, but smaller teams |
| **Shared resources** | Design system (Figma/Zeroheight) | Design Hub + Content Hub + Insights Hub |
| **Discovery** | PM + Designer research | Orchestrator + Insights Hub (AI-augmented) |
| **UI/UX creation** | Designer manually | Orchestrator + Design Hub (AI-generated, human-validated) |
| **Copy & communication** | Ad hoc, per-feature | Content Hub (automated consistency) |
| **Delivery** | Sprint-based | Continuous, AI-accelerated |

### 4.2 How It Works in Practice

Let's imagine a concrete scenario: **The Growth Orchestrator wants to redesign the onboarding flow for new users.**

1. **Queries the Insights Hub:** "What are the main drop-off points in onboarding? What are users saying in exit surveys?" The hub synthesizes data from analytics and qualitative research and returns a preliminary validation.

2. **Based on insights, defines hypotheses and prompts the Design Hub:** "Generate 3 onboarding flow variants for users coming from mobile acquisition, using our components and pattern library."

3. **The Design Hub generates variants** within the design system. The Orchestrator selects and refines. The **Content Hub** automatically proposes copy for all states and flags necessary updates to the FAQ.

4. **The Orchestrator and tech lead drive implementation** (AI-augmented coding) — they validate rather than build from scratch. The prototype is rapidly tested with users (Reforge Research style).

5. **Test results flow back into the Insights Hub** as new knowledge for the entire product.

### 4.3 Roles and Competencies in the New Model

| Role | Key Competencies | Relationship to Hubs |
|------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Orchestrator** | Product strategy, design sense, technical literacy, prompt engineering, outcome ownership | Primary user of all three hubs. Issues briefs, validates outputs, drives the cycle. |
| **Tech Lead** | Architecture, code review, AI-augmented development, system integration | Consumes Design Hub for implementation. Contributes technical constraints to all hubs. |
| **Hub Engineers** | Specialized engineers who maintain and improve the hubs themselves (DS, content pipelines, data infrastructure) | Own hubs as a product. Serve all Orchestrators. |
| **Design Lead** | Design governance, brand integrity, AI tool training, systems thinking | Owns the Design Hub. Defines rules and smart components. Trains Orchestrators. |
| **Research Lead** | User research methodology, data analysis, synthetic research | Owns the Insights Hub. Ensures data quality and validity of synthetic conclusions. |

## 5. Implementation Roadmap

### Phase 1: Foundations (Q2–Q3 2026)

- **Audit current workflows:** Map where teams spend time, identify repetitive tasks suitable for AI
- **Build the foundational Design Hub:** Convert the design system (tokens, components) into an AI-readable format (MCP, structured metadata)
- **Pilot the Insights Hub:** Connect feedback sources (support, NPS, analytics) into a single AI-analyzed system
- **Upskill the team:** Prompt engineering, AI prototyping (e.g., Reforge Build, Cursor), AI-augmented research

### Phase 2: Pilot Team (Q3–Q4 2026)

- **Select one domain team** (e.g., Growth) for the pilot Orchestrator model
- **Test the hub-based workflow end-to-end:** From insight through design to delivery
- **Launch the Content Hub** in a basic version: Tone of voice guidelines + AI-generated copy with human validation
- **Measure:** Discovery-to-delivery cycle speed, output quality, team satisfaction

### Phase 3: Scaling (2027)

- **Extend the Orchestrator model** to additional teams
- **Connect hubs** into an integrated orchestration layer (inspired by PwC Agent OS)
- **Add predictive capabilities:** Insights Hub predicts needs, Design Hub predicts breaking changes
- **Potentially increase the number of teams** (smaller, but more), because hubs eliminate duplicate work

## 6. Risks and Mitigations

| Risk | Description | Mitigation |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| **Output uniformity** | AI generators may produce generic design outputs | Design Lead as quality guardian; Human-in-the-loop validation; Defined guardrails in the Design Hub |
| **Loss of deep expertise** | Orchestrators may become "mile wide, inch deep" | Preserve specialized Hub Leads; Continuous learning program; Cagan's principle: the tool is not the skill |
| **Team resistance** | People may feel threatened by changing roles | Transparent communication; Training as an investment in people; Gradual rollout (pilot team) |
| **Hub as bottleneck** | If hubs can't keep up with team demands | Dedicated hub teams; Self-service access for Orchestrators; SLAs on hub responses |
| **AI output quality** | Hallucinations, bias, inconsistency | Automated validation; Red teaming; Feedback loops back into hubs |
| **The Klarna effect** | Overly aggressive replacement of humans with AI leading to quality decline | Augmentation, not substitution; Hub Leads as guardians; Gradual rollout; Measure quality, not just speed |

## 7. Conclusion

Research clearly shows that AI isn't just changing tools — it's changing how teams organize, how decisions are made, and how products are created. **Key consensus findings across all sources:**

- **Teams will shrink but gain greater scope and autonomy.** Gartner, SVPG, and Reforge all agree.
- **Centralized AI hubs/studios are the future.** PwC, Gartner, and Reforge confirm this from different angles.
- **Roles are converging, but not completely.** A new type of leader (the Orchestrator) will emerge, but deep expertise will remain critical.
- **Taste and intuition will become the key differentiator.** Skills will be increasingly commoditized by AI.
- **Design systems are becoming intelligent ecosystems.** This is the direct foundation for the Design Hub concept.
- **Augmentation wins over substitution.** Klarna showed what happens with the opposite approach.

> **Daniel's hub-based architecture model with Orchestrators isn't just a theoretical vision — it's in direct alignment with trends documented by PwC, Gartner, SVPG, Reforge, and others. Trezor has the opportunity to be an early mover in this transformation within the crypto industry.**
